UNITED KINGDOM
What we've learnt about the Brexit effect on the UK
<span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[15]/w_r[3]"/> <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[15]/w_r[3]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[15]/w_r[4]"/>Economists' w<span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[15]/w_r[4]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="3" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[15]/w_r[5]"/>arnings about 'no deal' Brexit have led to accusations of another 'Project Fear'…<span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="3" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[15]/w_r[5]"/>

Too much pessimism?
<span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[22]/w_r[1]"/>Warnings about 'no deal' Brexit have been dubbed by some commentators, including<span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[22]/w_r[1]"/>
former Brexit Secretary David Davis, as 'Project Fear 2.0' (BBC, 28 November 2018). 
The idea is that, just as forecasts for UK growth following June 2016's Brexit vote proved too downbeat, so are predictions of the fallout from 'no deal' Brexit.
True, economists were too gloomy about the aftermath of the vote in 2016. 
But the past year or two offers some redemption. 
While the initial slowdown didn't happen, predictions of sluggish growth from mid-2017 onwards have broadly come true.
Four surprises since June 2016

We think there have been two big upside surprises to the drivers of growth since 2016:<span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="3" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[29]/w_r[3]"/>
<span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[30]/w_r[1]"/> <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[30]/w_r[1]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[30]/w_r[2]"/>The initial uncertainty-related hit to investment and household spending was surprisingly mild. 
Most strikingly, the household saving ratio fell sharply. 
But business spending has remained weak, pointing to a slow-burn uncertainty drag.<span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[30]/w_r[2]"/>
<span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[1]"/> <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[1]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[2]"/>World <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[2]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="3" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[3]"/>– <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="3" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[3]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="4" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[4]"/>particularly eurozone <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="4" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[4]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="5" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[5]"/>– <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="5" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[5]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="6" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[6]"/>growth has been much stronger than expected.<span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="6" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[31]/w_r[6]"/>
Without this, UK growth might have been materially weaker.
There have been two big disappointments though:
<span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[35]/w_r[1]"/> <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[35]/w_r[1]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[35]/w_r[2]"/>Net trade has flat-lined, despite sterling weakness and eurozone strength.<span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[35]/w_r[2]"/>

Overall, we think the drop in sterling has had a net negative impact on UK growth.<span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[37]/w_r[2]"/>
Lessons for a 'no deal' Brexit
<span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[40]/w_r[1]"/>We think there has been a tangible drag on the economy from Brexit, before it has even happened. 
Granted, the <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="1" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[40]/w_r[1]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[40]/w_r[2]"/>initial <span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="2" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[40]/w_r[2]"/><span class="xml-tag docx-tag open-tag" data-id="3" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[40]/w_r[3]"/>uncertainty effect was modest, and global tailwinds masked some of the fallout. 
But we think a big headwind has built up over time, via weak investment, the inflation squeeze and disappointing net exports.<span class="xml-tag docx-tag close-tag" data-id="3" data-tag="w_r" data-xpath="/w_document/w_body/w_p[40]/w_r[3]"/>
In the event of a 'no deal' Brexit, these headwinds would almost certainly intensify. 
And of course, unlike in 2016, 'no deal' would involve tangible economic disruption at a time when the world economy is likely to be much less supportive. 
So unlike in 2016, we think 'no deal' would lead to a UK recession.

We have seen two upside and two downside surprises to the drivers of growth
We think these more accurate growth forecasts for 2017 and 2018 partially redeem economists' over-pessimism about late-2016. 
Chart 2 cumulates errors made by consensus and the Bank of England in their immediate post-referendum forecasts. 
It suggests that, although the level of UK GDP is still more than 1% higher than expected by consensus in September 2016, the cumulative error is no wider now than it was in early 2017. 
In other words, some of the post- referendum gloominess among economists, at least for 2017 and 2018, was well-founded.

Global growth has been stronger than expected
The second upside surprise on the growth front since mid-2016 has been in the external environment, eurozone growth in particular. 
Strikingly, the upside surprise to eurozone growth (relative to Consensus Economics forecasts made in September 2016) has been larger than for UK growth (Chart 7 versus Chart 1).
Stronger eurozone growth might have masked Brexit- related headwinds…
We think this acceleration in eurozone growth would have provided material support to the UK economy via trade and confidence. 
In its absence, UK growth might otherwise have been weaker and more in line with economists' forecasts. 
This would also chime with the notion that Brexit related headwinds did indeed weigh on the UK economy but, at least to some extent, they were masked by global tailwinds.
Net exports have disappointed
Given the strengthening in global demand, combined with the post-referendum drop in the pound (now 15% below late-2015 levels, in trade weighted terms), it would have been reasonable to expect a material improvement in net exports.
…but despite strong eurozone demand, UK net trade has been weak
In the event, net trade has not made any gains at all. 
Export growth has been remarkably lacklustre and has not managed to outstrip import growth (Chart 8). 
One possibility is that given the underlying level of economic uncertainty, business did not invest as much as they normally would to expand capacity to meet export demand, raising prices instead of output. 
Another is that competitiveness gains were offset by a shift in foreign demand away from the UK, given the prospect of post-Brexit trade barriers.

The income squeeze outweighed the net trade boost from the weaker pound
The sterling depreciation was net negative for growth
Taking (3) and (4) together, it appears that the 2016 sterling depreciation provided hardly any boost to net exports, but acted as a major drag on household incomes. 
While economists tend to think that exchange rate depreciations act as a form of stimulus, we think this decline in the pound has turned out to be (significantly) net negative.
